INTRODUCTION: 

 

Here is an important question. Can parents begin drug prevention too early? One person answered: parents should begin as soon as it is known that a baby is to be born”. But unfortunately to the answer above, past decades have described the growing problem of drug abuse as a problem of the youths. One of the consequences of this position is that, less attention has been given to children and other people in their post-youth stage of life. It is important however to note that life does not begin at a youthful stage neither does it terminate at it.

What happens at other stages of life are also very important especially the formative and developmental stages of life. Research has shown that, successfully raising drug–free children remain one of the cornerstones for finally producing drug–free youths. When children are raised in such a way that they can confidently say no to drugs, it helps them to maintain these ground for the rest of life. In raising such productive children, no magic wand is needed what is important on the part of parents is simple knowledge of facts on drugs and necessary drug prevention skills.

On the part of children, what is needed is the power of quick skill acquisition, practice and determination to be drug –free, which can be seen as a matter of choice of life style.

 

In their opinion on who confidently say no to drugs, Elizabeth, George and Katie in Lorraine Hendricks (1989) expressed the following opinions:

 

  • Although the children feel social stress (pressure to abuse drugs), they are mature enough to see the sources of this stress objectively.
  • Even if they have tried alcohol and other drugs, they don’t have a strong craving to repeat the experience.
  • The children are self-confident. They seem to be accomplishing something and they feel good about themselves. They feel they make their own decisions about drugs and other issues of life.
  • When an occasion to drink or take drugs arises, they have no problem saying no.
  • The children have goals such as playing games, educational pursuits etc. These goals are known not to go together with drugs.
  • They are aware that younger children look up to them. 
  • They are open to experiences that give them a natural high such as the weekend retreats where they go to know kids from other cliques. 
  • They are able to imagine themselves as parents teaching their children explicitly by example, how to live without chemically induced highs.                                                                                                                                                                                                    

The drug-free children described above display a complex interplay of potentials found in every other child including those who choose to abuse drugs.

What makes the differences between those who say no to drugs and those who say yes to the latter have been waylaid from the path fully matured?

In other words their inborn potentials are hindered from full development. In order to raise drug-free children therefore, parents should deploy every machinery to stimulate the growth of the inborn potentials of their children towards full maturity. This can be done by creating a ‘conductive’ home environment and teaching children the necessary life skills to saying no to drugs. Fortunately, this dual mandate above constitutes the soul of this book. This book therefore is a resource material for all homes and institutions dealing with children.

The Tabula–Rasa Concept and Enabling:

 

In discussing about raising drug-free children, it is paramount at the beginning stage to know that the role of parents is very significant. It has been said that the mind of a newborn child is a tabula –rasa i.e. a blank slate. First writers on this blank slate are family members especially the parents (mother and father). In this way, parents represent significant figures whose attitude and behavior towards their children becomes one of important determinant factor for raising children who can say no or yes to drugs. Before we proceed, let us assess our parenting attitude and behavior on the instrument designed by Wilmes (1988). Your response to the questions below should be either yes or no.

  3. I sometimes make excuses to school officials to keep my child out of trouble by saying he/she was sick when he/she skipped school

  2.               I often feel confused about what it means to be a parent.

If majority of your responses are yes, you might have good reasons and intentions aimed at keeping your child happy and at peace with you. However, these actions are in conflict with raising up drug free children. This takes us to a discussion of enabling.

 Enabling is defined by professionals of substance abuse as a process whereby well –meaning parents unwittingly allow and even encourage irresponsible and destructive behavior in their children by shielding them from the consequences of their behavior.

Here is a story of Shehu and his son Kunle to illustrate better what we are saying about enabling:

Kunle had some behavior problem in school but his involvement in hockey seemed to settle him down somewhat. Lately however, he started acting defiant again. Early In the year, the coach explained the state athletic code that forbids any use of drugs and of course Kunle promised to observe it.  A few weeks later, Shehu, out for a walk, came upon Kunle and some friends drinking beer in a car park. Shehu was angry but Kunle made up a story: When his friends asked him to go for a ride, he had no idea that they had beer in the car. He refused persistently to join them to a few beers but finally under pressure gave in. But it was the first time he had ever taken beer. The real story was that Kunle and his friends had been drinking beer together for several months. They either slip a few cans of beer out of the house or get an older friend to buy it for them at a liquor store.

Skeptical at first, Kunle’s father swallowed the whole story, thought it over and decided to say nothing to the coach or anyone else about it. After all, what if Kunle should get suspended from the team? And what if one little incident like that could even end up wrecking a possibly brilliant career in professional hockey. No need to tell the wife either because she may make a big deal out of it or worry herself to death. In the long run too, the little secret favor would make Kunle to think more highly of him.

Lesson from the story

 The story is a big lesson to parents. By protecting Kunle from the consequences of his unwise choice to drink beer contrary to his promise, Shehu has enabled him (encourage him) to make future irresponsible choice. After all Kunle boasted that nothing has happened.

 Furthermore, it is important to note that Shehu’s silence has weakened family ties. Keeping the mother in the dark over the matter prevented her from sharing her insight into the problem. Finally, what seems to be a cheap way for Shehu to get on the good side of an adolescent turned Kunle lately into a difficult person to handle.

What are your common grounds?    

 From the story, we can see that, many parents are a contributory factor to the wrong choices their children make in life.

 It is however hard for parents to recognize that their actions constitute ENABLING and even when their attention is drawn to it, they often deny because the truth is bitter and nobody is actually willing to accept responsibility for a problem behavior. But the earlier we examine our feelings, beliefs, and action critically as likely enablers, the better it will help us in raising drug- free children. The examination will be based on how our thoughts, beliefs and behaviors could unknowing be contributing to drug use by our children.

Examining our feelings: As Enablers

 

 Every parent shows great concern for their children. There is always a feeling of discomfort when our children are troubled. Some parents go to the extent of discomforting themselves so that their children will be comforted. But if our feelings should be at variance with sound values and judgment, and if we don`t express our sound feelings in words or behavior, this can result to enabling. For instance, many parents can express (in words) their feelings for the wrong behavior of their children but they cannot express themselves face to face with the children who are the chief custodian of those mal-adaptive behaviors.

 Examples of how our feelings can contribute to enabling can be seen from the following parenting behaviors:

  1. Instinct of parental over - protection: According to Wilmes (1988)

Parents would naturally want to protect their children from danger, but the instinct of over - protection can easily go astray. For instance, when on detention for allegedly writing obscenities on a classroom blackboard, Jumai told her father that she didn’t do it but she actually did. Her father stormed the school and threatened to take Jumai out of the school if she will not get “fair treatment”. Why did he act that way? For fear that his daughter was being mistreated, he angrily over - reacted and attempted to rescue her without really knowing what had happened. Such rescue attempts prompted by unresolved fear and anger, foster irresponsibility in children and this constitute an enabling behavior.

2. Fear: Unrealistic fear of depending doom can be an enabler to a drug – using behavior. For instance, Shade ran away from home when she was 10 years. Now, when she doesn’t get her way, she threatens to leave and the fear of what will happen to her motivate her parents to give in to her demand irrespective of what the demand is. This unrealistic fear becomes an enabler for her to learn irresponsibility.

3. Performance Anxiety: It is common today to hear children say: Daddy and Mummy will kill me if I fail to do this. Parents too are faced with thoughts in their places of work and other transactions expectation, which makes parents feel anxious, drains their energy. This makes such parents difficult to live with. Children brought up in such homes can hardly be relaxed to make right judgments. They may turn to drugs to feel relaxed.

4. Self-doubt: Parents who are not sure of their abilities and things they do are highly prone to make wrong decisions. Children under them can learn unwise choices including choices on drug use. Parent’s Self-confidence and faith in what they do is very important.

Examining our beliefs as enablers:

 

Beliefs can be defined as strong feelings that something is good. Our beliefs are formed through religion, culture, experience, education etc. Beliefs held by parents can become to children enablers for drug-using behavior. Such beliefs held by parent include the followings: 

1. My children will not listen to me: Some parents have reached a conclusion that their children can never listen to them. For this reason, they no longer talk to their children but allow them to take any decision. Research has shown that one of the key questions that come to the mind of children when they want to take decision to start using drugs is: What shall be the response of my parents? If parents believe that they can do something about decisions taken by their children, this can help in shaping decision and behavior of the children. Children who believe that their parents can do something about the decision they take will always strive to take decisions that will attract approval from their parents.

2. If everybody is doing it, it must be okay: This belief is wrong. Some parents allow their children to do what other children are doing because they are living in the same environment and other parents have allowed their children to do so. When children are trained under such conditions, it becomes difficult to control them when they follow other children to engage in some behaviors that apparently contradict the values of the parents. To avoid this situation, family values should be taught to children.

 

3. If I love my children, I must always trust them: As parent, it is imperative for us to love our children at all times, but we should not trust our children at all times. When they are deviating from what is normal, we should suspect and check them very well. If we permit love and trust to always go together in our relationship with our children, they will capitalize on that to engage in some vices with the belief that we will never suspect them.

 

Examining behaviors as enablers:

 

Behaviors are stronger enablers than feelings and beliefs because they are external and can be seen. Behaviors that can serve as enablers include the following:

1. Preventing children from facing the consequences of their behavior: When parents are in the habit of intervening to prevent their children from receiving the desired consequences of their wrong behavior, such children see that as a cover and progress to do even more. When their parents are fed up and are not willing to defend them any more, they accuse their parents of hating them. In view of this, parents should at the initial stage allow their children to face the due consequences of any unwanted behavior exhibited.

 

  1. Covering misbehavior of children: When parents are in the habit of covering the evil deeds of their children, this gives them the boldness to go ahead and commit more grievous atrocities. The belief is that whatsoever that is done will not be brought to public. This becomes an enabler, and lead children to commit atrocities that are self-announcing. Parents should therefore avoid covering the misbehavior of their children.

 

3. Doing children’s work for them: Every child should have a duty in the home to build his spirit of responsibility. When parents are in the habit of taking over the duties of their children on account of negligence on the part of the children, the children tend to feel that their contribution to the family is not a must. For this reason, they engage in behaviors that are not useful to the family.

Parents should cause their children to believe that their little contribution has a great effect on the survival of the family.

 

4. Allowing children always to have their way: There are reasons why parents allow their children to have their way. It could be that they don’t want to see their children unhappy, to avoid conflict with their children and the like. When children discover this weakness, they use it as a weapon against their parents to always have their way. Parents should therefore be careful.

Parents should thoroughly check the family environment for enablers. When this is taken care of, a good atmosphere for raising drug free children is already created.

 

CHAPTER TWO

 

Discipline That Produce a Drug Free Child 

 

         There are different forms of discipline, but this is a discipline that produces a drug -free child. The discipline that produces a drug – free child is our major concern in this book. In raising drug – free children, Parent should avoid conflicting opinions and actions vis-à-vis the discipline of their children. When there is division between parents over the direction of discipline, children tend to believe that they are hated by the parent who does not allow them to always have their way. The feeling of being hated by a parent sometimes makes such children to adopt deviant ways of coping with the situation which might be drug abuse. It is therefore important for parents to have a common voice over the discipline of their children. This is to prevent children from the adoption of wrong conclusion and maladaptive coping strategies to deal with the situation.

 Furthermore parents should adopt the following strategies as part of discipline to raise drug–free children:

 

Parents should set limits for their children: When limits are set, children normally try to test the set limits. This however should not prevent parents from setting limits. When limits are not set, children grow with a notion of a free world where one does whatever that is appealing to them. But this is dangerous because it is not everything that is appealing to a child that is right in the sight of the laws of the society.

In setting limits however, care should be taken to ensure that limits set are without ambiguity, and are appropriate. Furthermore, limits set must be enforced.

 

Unambiguous Limits: Limits set must be straightforward and clear to the understanding of the children. Both parents and children should have a common understanding, of intentions and goals of the set limits. Parents can achieve this by discussing the set limits with their children. To avoid forgetting the set limits by the children, parents should write them down and place them where children can see it on daily basis. If possible, children should be taught to commit the set limits to memory and mediate on them from time to time. Furthermore, children should from time to time be given a knowledge test to see whether they know the set limits of their families.

Appropriate limits: Limits set must be appropriate to age and meet the basic human needs of children. When this is not taken into consideration, violation of the set limits is common. On the other hand, children who insist on observing such dehumanized set limits deprive themselves of some human rights, which sometimes result to their physical, social and psychological under development. For instance, it will be proper to limit the movement of a child of less than 3 years to a family compound, but this will not be proper to an adolescent whose developmental need involves interaction with the wider society, becoming independent, becoming industrious etc. it is important for parents to respect the needs of their children because this makes children to develop self-respect and this goes a long way in avoiding acts that can result to the abuse of their persons. This is like the law of harvest. When parents sow respect and dignity in the life of their children, such children grow up feeling respected and dignified and will tend to value respect and dignity as well. This will help them to avoid acts that can attract disrespect and humiliation.

In setting limits, care should be taken to safe–guard the life of the children and limits should be set in such a way that their violation does not bring much harm to the children.             

 

Enforcement of limits: 

 

 When limits are set, there should be a commitment on the part of the parents to enforce the limits. When limits set are not enforced, the whole thing becomes child’s play. In enforcing limits, parents should act as examples.

Parents should avoid the slogan of: “Do as I say and not as I do” If the set limit includes alcohol, nobody in the family should use alcohol. In order to ensure compliance to the set limits, parents should check on the issues concerned from time to time. This send signals to the children that their parents are serious. The checking however should not be on daily basis. This is to avoid conclusion by children that their parents have no single iota of trust in them.

Setting Consequences: 

 

Children should be made to understand that to every behavior, there is a consequence, which could either be pleasant or unpleasant. Children should be made to understand that the positive or negative reward is from their parents but it’s an extension of what originates from nature. Instances of how nature punishes and rewards behaviors should be made known to children e.g. if you put hands into the fire to get warmth, it will burn you. But if you just stay close to the fire for warmth, the fire will give you the needed warmth.

    

QUALITIES OF EFFECTIVE CONSEQUENCES 

For consequences to be effective in the art of any child up-bringing, the consequences must possess the following qualities:

1. They must be reasonable: Consequences should always exceed the offence. This is to create an impact of the consequence of the violators.

2. They must be related to the offence: by applying this principle, if a child steals food, the child should be denied his normal ration of food sometime. If it is coming home late, his outing time should be denied.

3. Consequences: Consequences must be set in advance and clearly understood. When consequences are defaulting, children know what to expect. This prevents the explanation of consequences informs of calculated attempts by parents to unjustly punish them.

4. They must be timely: When limits are violated, it is better to punish immediately. This helps children to easily associate the consequence of wrong behavior.

5. They should be applied consistently: When a wrong behavior is punished sometimes and the same behavior rewarded or overlooked at other times, this creates confusion in the psyche of children, and consequences of such a behavior will loose their impact and the wrong behavior will hardly be corrected.

6. Consequences must be applied with love: When applying consequences to a wrong behavior, it is better to apply lovingly. This could be achieved by telling the child that he/she just has to bear the consequences of their behavior as a natural phenomenon.

 

Children should be brought up in such a way that they properly establish a link between behavior and consequences. When children are conscious of the fact that to every action, there is a consequence, they are better motivated to make choices that can produce pleasant consequences. Nowadays, it is common to see that when children are faced with the consequence of their behavior, their reactions clearly shows that they only considered what they wanted to do without giving thought to the consequences. There is no link between behavior and consequences (B-C) to them. Children who grow with the B-C missing link are more prone to abuse drug than those who grow with the B-C link.

Good as it may sound to set consequences to guide the behavior of children, care must be taken to ensure that setting consequences do not involve threats and promises. Threats come in form of: ‘If you don’t do this, I will kill you, bury you alive etc.’; Promises come in form of: ‘If you do this, I will buy a bicycle, car, biscuit etc.’ The question is how many of these threats and promises are actually fulfilled. I for one have never seen a parent burying the child alive because of what the child has failed to do even though I have heard many parents threaten to do so. The non-fulfillment of these threats and promises make children not to take their parents serious. This is why it is important for parents to be moderate in the design of the consequences and to ensure that they are religiously carried out.

FAMILY SPIRIT:

Raising children with family spirit helps them to be drug-free. To ensure family spirit in children there must be love, good interaction, sharing and concern among the members. In as it is good to have this in place in every family, social changes has made this difficult to achieve. Instead of seeing a united family today, we see a group of individuals living in the same house but all pursuing their personal and isolated agendas. This is so in order to cope with the financial, political, religious and civic demands placed on members of the family. In the mist of such a scattered situation, children may lack the family spirit of love, togetherness and parental support. Consequently, they will look for these from deceptive groups and high feelings they get when they take drugs.

 Demanding schedules are things we can hardly escape. But looking at the consequences they have on our children, there is need to take measures that can address the situation. Some measures include:

1. Family Schedules: It is essential to set up a weekly calendar outlining where every member of the family will be and when they will be back home. This makes it easier in terms of physical contact when the need arise. This gives more of a sense of security to the children too.

2. Family Activities: It is good to schedule activities that all family members are expected to attend e.g. church services, devotion etc.

3. Family meetings: Time should be created periodically when all members of the family will freely discus events. Group and individual problems and success should be brought up for discussions at such meetings

4. Housework schedule: It is necessary for every member of the family to have some work to do. Things to be done should be done without expecting any remuneration. Children learn how to contribute to the society by practicing similar contributions at home.

5.  House – Utilization Plan: It is proper to educate children on the activities associated with the various parts of the house and when they are expected to be there. A consistently wrong presence or use of any household unit is a signal that something is wrong with the children. For instance, while it is only proper for a child to be found in the Garden during the day time, a drug using child may be consistently found in garden even in the night in order to hide his identity and smoke.

 

TEACHING CHILDREN TO ACCEPT RESPONSIBILITY

  Parents can raise drug free children by teaching them to know that they are responsible for their feeling, behavior, school performance, and social life, consequences from things that happen outside the home, their future and the like. Unfortunately parents push their children aside and take responsibilities that their children are supposed to take for a healthy social and personality development. Parents should therefore be careful in the way and manner they intervene in the following life aspect of their children.

1. Feeling: Parent should avoid the tendency or temptation to always rescue their children from their feelings. Children should be allowed to walk through their feelings. This will teach them that in life or in relationships: two feeling are expected i.e. pleasant and unpleasant feelings. If they enjoy pleasant feelings, they should therefore make conscious efforts to make choices that can result to pleasant feeling.

Furthermore, parents should teach their children to know that the world cannot change just because of the way they feel. This should be made clear to children because when children want to get or do something, which their parents are not comfortable with it, they use negative feelings (sadness, anger) against their parents to have their way. By putting on these feelings, they are responsible for the way they are feeling. That if their request is granted, the feelings will disappear. Parents feel guilty for these feelings and permit their children to do or to have what can send away negative feelings. This is wrong. Children should be taught to believe that the world cannot change because of their feelings.

Behavior: Parents should cause their children to know and believe that they are responsible for their behaviors and so should be blamed in case of any situation. Unfortunately, parents often blame themselves and others for the behavior of their children. For instance fathers sometimes blame mothers for the wrong behavior of children. In such situations, what is supposed to be said to the child is not but said to the mother. If a child believes that another person is held responsible for his behavior, this becomes an enabler to him.

Consequences from acts outside the home: In as much as it is necessary for parents to watch out to see that their children are not unjustly treated as they engage in activities outside the home, parents should avoid buying the consequences of their children’s behavior as they interact with the outside world. For instance, using money or social influence to bail a child who vandalized public property is wrong. This parental act registers negative information in the psyche of children. The simple and common information registered is that: ‘I am above the law’. Unnecessary parental defense is damaging. Children should be allowed to defend their actions to the best of their knowledge and abilities.

KNOWING FACTS ABOUT ALCOHOL, TOBACCO AND OTHER DRUGS (ATOD): Parents need to have good knowledge on drugs so as to help raise drug free children. This should include knowledge on: types of drugs and associated side effects: identification of paraphernalia associated with each drug, street names of drugs, what different drugs look like, signs for drug abuse, how to get help to a child suspected to be abusing drugs etc. When parent are armed with the above information, they are better placed to identify easily when a child is initiated into drug abuse and to take more appropriate steps that can rescue the child from the deadly hands of drug abuse.  

DISCUSSING WITH CHILDREN:  To raise drug free children, parents must from the habit of discussing drug issues with their children. It is however, unfortunate to note that many parents shun the idea of discussing drug issues with their children. Many believe that by discussing drug issues with their children, they will raise drug use interest in the children. To other parents, it is ignorance that affects this discussion. Some parents prefer to talk on drug issues with their   children as if their children will take to drug use.  But what is important to know here is that, before you know, as a parent, that your child is taking drugs, the child might have gone far. This is why it is good to talk to children early enough for preventive measures.

1. Good and active listening: For parent’s discussion to affect their children, they must be good listeners. Good listening encourages children to talk more. By good listening, areas of conflicts and hidden areas will be spotted and explored further. A good listening parent can create a conducive atmosphere that can encourage their children to always bring in problems for discussion. Parents should not ignore or frustrate the desire of their children for discussion when sensitive issues are involved or mentioned. When listening skills are lacking, parents, should find ways of learning them.

2. Encourage your child: In talking to children on drug issues, it is important to talk and encourage them on areas they are doing well. Children build confidence in themselves and their parents when they encourage them. Parents should therefore not be seen by their children as fault finders only.

3. Transmission of simple and clear information: In talking to children about drugs, the information passed to them should be simple and clear. This will make it easier for them to understand what is being said. For instance, “in our family, we don’t allow the use of drugs that are illegal, and children are not allowed to drink alcohol or smoke cigarette”. This information is quite simple & straightforward.

4. Be a good example: children learn easier and faster through modeling, i.e. by observing and copying what others are doing. On this note, parents who want their children to be drug free should themselves be drug-free.

5. Take note of developmental changes: In talking to children about drug issues, parents should note that from one stage of development to another, developmental needs and concerns differ. If taken into consideration that information passed to children at certain stages of their life will not have any beneficial values to them then that information should be with-held. Let us therefore briefly look at some of the stages of development and the types of information that will be required to produce effect in the lives of children:

a. Children between the ages of 2-4 years: Children at this age cannot listen quietly for a very long time. They cannot comprehend complex facts, and are more interested in doing for themselves. A learn - by- doing approach can therefore be used at this development stages to teach children how to be skilled in taking decisions against drugs.

Parents can help children by doing the following:

i. Tell children all the harmful substances that are available in the house. Tell them the dangers associated with each and keep them away from the reach of children.

ii. Tell children how medicine can be harmful if not used properly. Tell them not to swallow any tablet or drink any liquid from any bottle except when given to them by parents or other specific people.

iii. Tell children about the need for good things, food etc. and why they should introduce them into their bodies. You can ask your children to name the good foods they know.

iv. Encourage your child to follow instructions. For instance you can ask your child to assist you in doing things that are procedural. You can also engage in games with rules with your children.

v. Teach your child decision making by arranging some situations and asking him to make choices. Even if the choice made is not correct, encourage the child. It is good to let the child know that you think he can make some good decision

vi. Set out regular time to interact and play with your children. This could be reading, walking, and eating together. This creates a bond of trust and affection between parents and children. Children with positive self-concept are less likely to say yes to drugs.

b.  Children between the ages of five (5) to nine (9) years: Children at this stage learn by experience, and don’t have a good understanding of things that will happen in the future. At this stage of development, facts and fantasy are easily mixed. The world is seen as the child wishes it to be, and not as the world actually is. The world therefore can be described as a wishful world.

At this development, discussions on drugs should be related with people, events and things the child knows. Since most children at this stage are interested in how their bodies work, discussion should be focused on avoidance of things that can harm the full development and normal functioning of their bodies. Parents can engage in the following activities to help their child better:

  1. Practice ways to say no with your child. Here, parents can create artificial situations and ask their children to respond to it.
  2. Let your child know the difference between drugs and medicine
  3. Explain to your child how advertisement can easily persuade somebody to do something.

Talk them.

Children at this age are committed to friends. What they think is determined by what their friends think and do. This makes it difficult for children to make independent decisions. This stage of development therefore becomes a crucial one that calls for serious drug prevention efforts. At this stage, children need factual information on drug and strong motivation to resist peer pressure to use drugs.

Parents can engage in the following activities to help their children at this stage of development:

  1. Have a good time with your children. This involves reading, playing, walking about and eating together. This is good for formation of a healthy parent-child bond.
  2. Encourage your child to participate in activities where they interact and get into friendship with children of good behavior. For instance religious activities.
  3. Teach your child how advertisement can lure them easily into ways that are not accepted by the family.
  4. Practice how to say no with your children.
  5. Encourage your child to join clubs that are anti-drugs.
  6. Discourage your child from having excess free time. This is because an idle mind is the devil’s workshop.

d. Children in their early teens: this development stage refers to early adolescent. At this stage, children are struggling to find a new identity by throwing away friends, ideas etc. and acquiring new ones.

Children at this stage can begin to deal abstract things and the future. They understand that their actions have consequences and they know how their behavior affects others. Young people at this stage sometimes have a shaky self-image. They are not sure whether they are growing and changing adequately and they are often in conflict with adults. Strong emotional support therefore is important. At this age, many young people abuse drug because their friends abuse drugs. Prevention measures at this stage therefore should be focused and reinforcing your child’s motivation to avoid drugs.

Parents can engage in the following activities to help their children at this stage of development:

  1. Know the friends of your child as well as their parents. Share your vision with them as you get in touch with them
  2. Monitor the movement of your child. This involves the place he/she is going and with whom. It is also good to know the time he/she is expected back.
  3. Teach your child to recognize problem situations such as joints, lonely places etc.
  4. Help your child to develop a positive-self-image. This can be achieved by the provision of good food, clothes, and good education as well as unconditional acceptance of the child and encouragement.
  5. Tell your child who a good friend is. That is people that will not lure him into drug abuse.
  6. Discuss problem areas of life with your child, and insist in tackling the problems.
  7. Review periodically with your child family rules and assigned responsibilities. Encouraged him to make his input.

e. Children in their late teens: This period can be described as late adolescence. Young people at this age can engage in abstract thinking. They have an increasingly realistic understanding of adults. They therefore want adults to discuss their concerns and the ways they solve problems and make decisions. This becomes an open opportunity to help your child. Parents should use this opportunity to tell the children the long-term side effects of drugs. Other important things to do by parents include the following:

  1. Plan drug-free activities with other families during holidays. This is to handle the free and idle period of the holidays.
  2. Provide up-to date information about drugs to your children.
  3. Ensure that parties attended by your children are drug-free.

     Discuss future plans of your child with him. What they want to read and become in future (life).

 

 

CHAPTER THREE

Drug paraphernalia

 

Tools of the Trade

 

Being a parent is tough, but the job becomes tougher once those teenage years roll around. Though “popular” or “trendy” drugs of abuse change, their paraphernalia items stay the same…and we can use that to our advantage. The sooner we recognize signs of drug abuse, the sooner we can intervene.  (Credit: American Addiction Center)

 

Tools of the Trade is a blog series designed to empower parents to accurately identify drug paraphernalia and take action.

 

Many parents don’t like (or want) to play the role of family policeman – snooping, interrogating and punishing their teenagers. However, as parents, it is our job to protect and serve.

 

If you believe your teen might be using cocaine, it’s your job as a parent (not a cop) to intervene. After all, we’re talking about a drug that can cause severe psychological and physical effects, especially after prolonged use. And none of us want a future like that for our children.

 

Drug paraphernalia is any equipment used to produce, consume, and conceal illicit drugs. Identifying paraphernalia can be a challenge, as many of these products are marketed as items for some legitimate purpose. As such, to recognize paraphernalia, a person needs to look at the context and the manner in which an item has been used or sold.

 

 How to Spot Cocaine Paraphernalia 

To intervene, you first need to know what to look for. While white residue is a pretty obvious sign of cocaine use, other paraphernalia can be a little trickier to spot. Here’s a look at those red flag items:

 

  • Money Bills and Hollow Pens: Rolled-up money bills and hollow pens are both common pieces of paraphernalia. If your teen is sniffing cocaine, residue can normally be found on either delivery device. Also, other parts of a dissembled pen may be a sign of use. While your teenager may be careful to conceal the hollow tube, he may careless with discarding the tips or the ink cartridges. And remember, a rolled-up bill doesn’t automatically unroll after use. In other words, a loosely curved bill left on a table may be red flag for cocaine use.

 

  • Snuff Bullets A small device traditionally used for tobacco snuff (in the 19th century), “snuff bullets” are almost exclusively used today as a way to snort cocaine and other drugs. They are small plastic, acrylic or metal cylinder devices that deliver portioned cocaine (a bump) to the user. They feature a screw-on bottom for filling the bullet and a lever-like device on the side for portioning the powder out.
  • CD Cases and Small Mirrors Other obvious and extremely common pieces of paraphernalia, CD cases and small mirrors are used to snort cocaine, namely by serving as a tray or plate where teens cut out lines of the drug. Even if he wipes the surface clean, residue can typically be found between the glass and the frame of a mirror. Also, if he’s using razor blades to cut lines, the glass may also feature scratches.

 

  • Strange Jewelry: Secret compartment jewelry is also something to consider. The jewelry is designed to be discreet, but as a parent, you have an idea of your teen’s fashion sense. In reality, jewelry designed to carry and conceal cocaine is anything but discreet. The pieces of jewelry are usually bulky and altogether unfashionable. In other words, if your child starts wearing strange lockets or bulky rings, they might be cocaine compartments – not questionable fashion statements.

 

  • Baggies and Bindles: Cocaine is typically packaged in small, re-sealable bags or tied with the cut, triangular corners of a larger plastic bag. However, you should also keep a look out for paper folded into a “bindle,” an origami-like method that uses paper (or money bills) as cocaine packaging. If you see papers with multiple creases in a strange design, they may have been bindles.

 

  • Razor Blades and Plastic Cards Both of these items are used for chopping and crushing rocky cocaine into a fine powder, making it easier to snort. While one loose plastic card may not be cause for concern, important cards – especially bank cards and IDs – aren’t typically left lying around. Also, there’s no reason your teen should own razorblades. If you spot blades or loose plastic cards in his room or car – and you notice a white residue on them – it’s time to talk about cocaine use.

 

How to Spot Crack Cocaine (Drug) and its Paraphernalia? 

5 Common Pieces of Crack Paraphernalia

 

If you’re concerned your teen may be abusing crack, you’ll want to learn about the following five pieces of paraphernalia traditionally associated with crack cocaine, such as what a crack pipe or “stem” looks like.

 

  • Light Bulbs and Crack Pipe:
    Crack does not light on its own (like marijuana or hash); it must be slowly heated to burn. While a crack user may use a traditional glass pipe, light bulbs are an incredibly popular, make-shift device for smoking crack. Users remove the bulb’s inner workers, place the crack inside the bulb, and light the outside of the glass. If you find a bulb with burn marks or empty light bulbs, it’s important to intervene quickly.
  • Steel Wool (Iron sponge), Faucet Aerators and Chore Boy:
    When someone does use a traditional pipe (or stem), they require a filter to keep the crack in place. Placing steel wool or copper Chore Boy, both scrubbing materials, inside the pipe is the most common method to do so. Faucet aerators are the screens obtained by screwing off the end of older faucets. These are also commonly used as screens for smoking crack.
  • Tin Foil and Straws
    Known as freebasing, using tin foil and a straw (or a hollow pen) is probably the second-most common method for smoking crack. Users make a crease in the foil, heat the crack from underneath, and use a straw to inhale the smoke. Finding balled up pieces of tin foil or tin foil with yellowish-brown or black burn markings may indicate your teen is using crack.
  • Plastic Bottles (Bunkers)/Aluminum Cans
    Another home-made, make-shift device using plastic bottles, locally called bunkers, involves getting a plastic bottle, filling it a quarter or half way with either water or alcohol, making a pen sized hole in the bottle side, putting an aluminum foil screen over the bottle mouth and using a pin to make multiple tiny holes in it. 

If you come across bottles with a single hole in the side, matchbox sized cut aluminium/tin foil with multiple tiny holes, most likely your teen is using crack cocaine. Also aluminum cans are also common. A user indents a side of the can, makes pin-sized holes in the indentation, places a screen over the holes, and lights the crack on the screen.

While finding a can in this form may indicate smoking other types of drugs (marijuana, hash, etc.), it’s no less a reason to initiate a conversation.

While marijuana and hash produce very poignant smells, crack’s smell is more-subtle yet still identifiable. Many describe it has burning plastic or cat pee.

 

  • Glass Pipes and Stems:
    While using a glass pipe or bong is common, “stems” are the preferred type of smoking device for crack. These are either straight, hollow glass tubes or glass tubes with a rounded, bulbous end. After prolonged use, the stem will have brown smoke residue inside and black burn marks on the outside end used for lighting the crack. If you find a stem, it’s crucial to confront your child and quickly make arrangements for treatment. 

 

How to spot Meth – Methamphetamine (ICE) and its Paraphernalia?

Drugs are incredibly strong these days, but few of them are more volatile than the stimulant drug methamphetamine. Created in make-shift “labs,” methamphetamine (crystal, meth, ice, glass, crank) is a Frankenstein-style drug made from the volatile chemicals found in batteries, fertilizer and engine starter. It can be smoked, injected or snorted, and can cause psychosis, strokes, coma and death.

Today’s teens are exposed to meth more than ever before, thanks to its acceptance as a cheap “party drug.”  

 Everything about crystal screams “deadly,” so it’s critical to be both educated and aware.

When it comes to meth paraphernalia, there are several items you might need to look out for if you believe a loved one is dealing with meth addiction, depending on the intake method. Meth can be snorted, smoked, ingested, or injected.2 Depending on that, you might find this paraphernalia:

  • Spoons are used for cooking meth by placing powdered methamphetamine in it and heating it with a lighter or some other flame source. You might recognize them by the burn marks.
  • Lighters are also a common sign of someone burning meth when in the presence of other tools.
  • Loose razor blades might be used to cut meth into lines before snorting.
  • Mirrors with straight scratch marks usually mean that a person used it as a chopping board for meth.
  • Straws or hollowed ballpoint pens can be used for snorting meth.
  • Bongs are commonly used to smoke marijuana but also meth. 
  • Additional shoelaces, an elastic lanyard, or a hairband might be used to plump up veins before injecting.

 

 

Common Meth (Methamphetamine) Paraphernalia

 

If you find any of the following paraphernalia, it’s time to talk to your teen about meth:

Glass and Home-Made Meth Pipes

  • Glass pipes can differ in design, but meth pipes are typically long, glass cylinders with rounded, bulbous ends.
  • A used pipe has black burn marks on the underside of the end.
  • Meth leaves a waxy, yellowish residue in the pipe after being smoked.
  • Light bulbs are a popular make-shift smoking solution. Teens empty the inner working parts and use the bulb as a smoking device. (Light bulbs show the same burn marks and residue.)

 

Meth Tin Foil and Aluminum Cans

  • Multiple pieces of creased tin foil can indicate meth abuse. Users place meth in the crease, heat the foil (to evaporate the drug) and inhale the smoke.
  • Similarly, users make indentations in aluminum cans and poke pin-sized holes. They place meth in the indentation and inhale the smoke through the opening of the can.
  • If you find blackened foils or suspicious cans, it’s vital to confront your child.
  • Cut Straws and Empty Ink Pens
  • Cut straws and empty ink pens are the preferred means of snorting meth.
  • Meth does not easily stick the plastic material, unlike a rolled-up money bill or other form of paper.
  • Cut straws and hollowed plastic pens are also popular inhalation devices when smoking meth using tin foil

 

Needles and Syringes

  • If your teen has no medical reason to possess hypodermic needles, finding this type of paraphernalia requires immediate action.
  • Finding needles generally indicates a teen is shooting either meth or heroin, both incredibly dangerous practices.
  • Shooting meth is a chemical onslaught to the bloodstream, posing the highest potential for an overdose.
  • The practice of using needles also comes with its own dangers, such as contracting HIV or hepatitis through needle sharing.

Sandwich Baggies

  • Meth is typically packaged in small plastic bags or baggies.
  • Residue seen inside the corners of baggies may appear as a crystalline powder, with an off-white or light brownish hue, or as chunky pieces of clear, broken glass known as shards.
  • Even a tiny amount of residue can be used to perform a drug addiction test (contact us if you need some), helping you positively identify the presence of crystal meth.

 

 

How to Spot Heroin and its Paraphernalia: (Foil, Spoons, Pipes & More)

Heroin can be ingested in many ways, although some methods cause the drug to affect the brain and body more rapidly than others

In its purest form, heroin is a white powder that may look like cocaine or pure amphetamines. Other varieties include a brownish powder and a black tar-like substance; color and texture changes indicate the introduction of impurities or additives.

Once drugs like heroin have impurities or additives in them, they need some special preparation methods to be effective. The more a drug requires preparation – through heat or dissolving in liquid, for example – the more paraphernalia will be associated with using the drug. The most common methods for using heroin include

  • Injecting it into a vein or muscle
  • Smoking it
  • Snorting it

Different paraphernalia is associated with these different methods of ingestion.

Paraphernalia Used to Prepare and Ingest Heroin

  • Injecting: People who inject heroin into a vein or a muscle get the most immediate effect of the drug because it directly enters the bloodstream and rapidly travels to the brain.3 People who inject heroin experience effects from the drug within five minutes. However, injection requires the most paraphernalia since is the most complicated preparation. Items used to prepare and inject heroin include:
    • Hypodermic needle: used to inject the liquefied drug into a vein or sometimes a muscle
    • Cotton balls: to strain the liquid drug and pull out chunks of impurities that did not melt
    • Spoons or bottle caps: vessels to “cook” the drug, or turn tar, solidified, or powdered heroin into a liquid for injection
    • Tie-off: often a shoelace, piece of rubber hose, or string that ties off a limb – usually an arm – and change blood flow to make veins pop out
    • Lighter or candle: the heat source used to melt heroin into a liquid
  • Smoking: Powdered heroin is sometimes smoked.4 Paraphernalia associated with smoking heroin include:
    • Aluminum foil: a surface used to contain the drug while it is smoked
    • Lighter or candle: a heat source placed under the aluminum foil, which causes the drug to liquefy and emit steam or smoke
    • Straw: used to inhale the smoke and steam as it wafts off the heroin on the aluminum foil
    • Cigarette, rolling papers, or pipe: when heroin is rolled into a cigarette or placed in a pipe to be smoked
  • Snorting: Powdered heroin can be snorted, especially if it is close to pure, to produce a strong euphoric effect. There is little paraphernalia or ritual around snorting heroin unlike with other drugs like cocaine. However, a person who snorts heroin is still likely to use a straw, rolled bill or paper, or another small tube to guide the powdered heroin into their nostrils.

 

CODEINE ABUSE & ADDICTION EFFECTS, SIGNS & SYMPTOMS

 

codeine addiction and substance use disorder 

Codeine, one of the most commonly used opioid narcotics in the world, is a relatively short-acting analgesic, with most effects lasting a few hours. A member of the opiate class of narcotic prescription medications, codeine is derived from a member of the opium poppy plant. Opiates include other drugs such as heroin, Vicodin, hydrocodone, OxyContin, and Demerol. Codeine is generally prescribed by a physician to treat mild-to-moderate levels of pain, manage a pesky cough, and occasional treat gastrointestinal problems such as diarrhea. Most individuals who are prescribed codeine to manage a medical illness take the drug directly as prescribed and never have a problem, but some individuals develop an addiction.

Used for centuries to manage pain in chronic pain sufferers, codeine and other opiates are becoming the drug of choice for many drug addicts. In fact, opiates are now the most commonly abused drug in the world. Opiates such as codeine are drugs of abuse due to the powerful effects on the user. In addition to pain relief, in higher doses codeine can lead to a pleasurable euphoric sensation. When opiates enter the central nervous system, they activate the reward system of the brain, allowing for excessive release of pleasurable hormones. It is this feeling that codeine addicts crave.

While many individuals do find that codeine is a miracle drug for the relief of symptoms, codeine addiction is no miracle. Like any addiction, codeine use and abuse can lead to devastating health and interpersonal problems. Many individuals who abuse codeine become tolerant to the mild effects of the narcotic and begin to abuse heavier and stronger narcotics to achieve greater highs. In addition, many individuals with codeine addiction begin to use other drugs such as benzodiazepines and alcohol in order to achieve greater highs. Others may abuse codeine and stimulant drugs in order to reduce the side effects of the stimulants and produce a more mellow high.

 

 Signs and symptoms of codeine addiction

The symptoms of codeine abuse are varied among addicts depending largely upon the length of time a person abuses codeine, the amount used, and the frequency of usage. Some of the most common symptoms of codeine abuse are:

Mood symptoms:

  • Depression
  • Anxiety
  • Emotional numbing
  • Euphoria
  • Sense of well-being and calmness
  • Mood swings

Behavioral symptoms:

  • Withdrawing socially from loved ones
  • Social isolation
  • Forging prescriptions in order to obtain more codeine
  • Doctor shopping, or visiting a number of doctors in order to obtain greater amounts of codeine
  • Frequent ER visits for vague complaints of pain
  • Stealing or borrowing codeine from friends and loved ones
  • Financial problems
  • Fixation on codeine
  • Apathy
  • Indifference toward loved ones, job, and social activities
  • Legal problems
  • Healthcare fraud
  • Ordering codeine on the internet
  • Faking illnesses to obtain more codeine
  • Lying to hide the amount of codeine being abused
  • Interpersonal relationship problems
  • Sleepiness
  • Poor work or school performance
  • “Nodding off” during conversations

Physical symptoms:

  • Blue tinges on the lips and fingernail beds
  • Dizziness
  • Fainting
  • Dry mouth
  • Constipation
  • Itching
  • Rashes
  • Urinary retention
  • Low blood pressure
  • Decreased sex drive
  • Respiratory depression
  • Seizures

Psychological symptoms:

  • Psychosis
  • Hallucinations
  • Delusions
  • Worsening of mental health
  • Increased mental illness symptoms
  • Decreased memory
  • Lack of emotions
